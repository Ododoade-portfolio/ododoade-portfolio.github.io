About Me

Turning Data into Decisions

I'm a data analyst with 8 years of experience working across operations, healthcare, and technology. My work sits at the intersection of data and decision making. I build pipelines, design dashboards, and dig into datasets to help organizations understand what is actually going on and what to do next. I have worked across the full data lifecycle, from cleaning and querying raw data to delivering reports and automations that teams actually rely on.

What keeps me going is pretty simple. I like solving problems. Not just finding the answer, but making sure the right people have it in a way they can actually use. I work well with teams, but I am also comfortable owning something end to end and figuring it out independently. For me, good data visualization is not just about the numbers. It is about what happens because of them.

Problem
A Boston PD dataset covering thousands of crime records from 2017 to 2020 had no structure to identify patterns across time, geography, or offense type, making it difficult to surface actionable insights.
Solution
Built a Tableau dashboard featuring hour of day trends, a major offenses treemap, a top 10 frequency chart, and monthly trend lines, making pattern recognition fast and visual.
Result
Found that crime peaked in afternoon and evening hours, larceny was the dominant offense, and incident volume stayed steady before a notable dip in 2020, supporting resource allocation decisions.

Problem
There was no clear picture of how institutional variables like tuition, graduation rates, and faculty credentials related to each other across private and public colleges, making cross sector comparisons difficult.
Solution
Built a multivariate comparison grid in R combining scatter plots, density curves, Pearson correlation coefficients, and box plots across six variables for both institution types side by side.
Result
Out of state tuition showed the strongest correlation with room and board costs, and private institutions consistently demonstrated stronger variable relationships than public ones, informing policy and benchmarking decisions.

Common questions

Frequently Asked Questions

What types of data projects do you specialize in? +

Mostly dashboard development, reporting automation, and data analysis. I work across the full pipeline, cleaning and structuring data, building the models or queries behind it, and then presenting it in a way that is easy for teams to actually use and act on.

How long does a typical dashboard project take? +

It really depends on the data. If everything is clean and the requirements are clear, I can turn around a solid dashboard in two to three days. If there is cleaning and structuring involved first, I factor that into the timeline upfront so delivery is always on schedule.

Can you work with data that's messy or unstructured? +

Yes, and honestly that is where a lot of the real work happens. Most data I have worked with needed cleaning before anything useful could be done with it. I am comfortable going in, figuring out what is there, and getting it to a point where it can actually be analyzed.

Do you provide ongoing support after a project is delivered? +

Yes. I do not just hand something off and disappear. I make sure whoever is using the dashboard or report actually understands it, and I am available if something needs to be adjusted or updated down the line.

Have you worked in healthcare or regulated industries? +

Yes, a significant part of my experience is in healthcare operations and compliance, where data accuracy and confidentiality are not optional. That background makes me careful and thorough with data regardless of the industry I am working in.

Can you train my team to use the dashboards or tools you build? +

Absolutely. There is no point building something nobody knows how to use. I make sure the people who need to use it actually feel comfortable with it before I consider the project done.
